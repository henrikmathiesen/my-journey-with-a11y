My journey with a11y

Principle Guideline Success Criteria Conformance Understanding How to Meet
1 Perceivable
1.1 Text Alternatives
1.1.1 Non-text Content A Make information conveyed by non-text content accessible through the use of a text alternative. Text can be read visually, read out loud by assistive technology or be converted to braile.
It helps: Visually impaired, hearing impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.1.1: Non-text Content 		Short text alternative, Long text alternative, Alternatives for controls & inputs, Alternatives for time-based media, exams, or specific sensory experiences, CAPTCHA, Decorations or elements meant to be ignored by AT
1.2 Time-based Media
1.2.1 Audio-only and Video-only (Prerecorded) A Make information conveyed by non-text content accessible through the use of a text alternative. Text can be read visually, read out loud by assistive technology or be converted to braile. For prerecorded video content, authors have the option to provide an audio track.
It helps: Visually impaired, hearing impaired, cognitive impaired(?)
WCAG: Understanding 1.2.1: Audio-only and Video-only (Prerecorded) 		Long text alternative (text transcript), or provide a link to an audio version of the video-only presentation
1.2.2 Captions (Prerecorded) A The intent of this Success Criterion is to enable people who are deaf or hard of hearing to watch audio and video presentations. Captions are similar to dialogue-only subtitles except captions convey not only the content of spoken dialogue, but also equivalents for non-dialogue audio information needed to understand the program content, including sound effects, music, laughter, speaker identification and location.
It helps: hearing impaired, cognitive impaired(?)
WCAG: Understanding 1.2.2: Captions (Prerecorded) 		If you provide a video with sound, provide captions. The captions should include both speech and any other sounds that provide significant information.
1.2.3 Audio Description or Media Alternative (Prerecorded) A People who are blind or visually impaired should be able to access the visual information on audio and video presentation.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired(?)
WCAG: Understanding 1.2.3: Audio Description or Media Alternative (Prerecorded) 		Long text alternative (text transcript), or a supplemental audio track, with audible explanation of what's happening on screen, placed during existing pauses in dialog
1.2.4 Captions (Live) AA People who are deaf or hard of hearing are enabled to watch real-time presentations if captions provide the part of the content available via the audio track.
It helps: hearing impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.2.4: Captions (Live) 		Provide captioning for live media. Captions should include both dialogue and important sounds.
1.2.5 Audio Description (Prerecorded) AA The audio description augments the audio portion of the media presentation. People who are blind or have low vision as well as those with cognitive limitations who have difficulty interpreting visually what is happening will benefit.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.2.5: Audio Description (Prerecorded) 		Provide an additional audio track for prerecorded video content describing what is happening on-screen.
1.2.6 Sign Language (Prerecorded) AAA Written text, such as that found in captions, is often a second language to people who are deaf or hard of hearing. This AAA success criteria promotes the use of sign language translation of audio.
It helps: hearing impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.2.6: Sign Language (Prerecorded) 		Including a sign language interpreter in the video stream and make sure the interpreter is clearly visible.
1.2.7 Extended Audio Description (Prerecorded) AAA This AAA criteria takes into considaration that sometimes audio description is not enough, it needs to be extended.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.2.7: Extended Audio Description (Prerecorded) 		The media is paused between scenarios/problems and the extended audio description plays.
1.2.8 Media Alternative (Prerecorded) AAA This AAA critera focuses on those whose vision is too poor to reliably read captions and whose hearing is too poor to reliably hear dialogue and audio description. The alternative for time-based media reads something like a book.
It helps: Visually impaired, hearing impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.2.8: Media Alternative (Prerecorded) 		Long text alternative
1.2.9 Audio-only (Live) AAA A live text caption service will enable live audio to be accessible to people who are deaf or hard of hearing, or who cannot otherwise hear the audio. Such services use a trained human operator who listens in to what is being said and uses a special keyboard to enter the text with only a small delay.
It helps: hearing impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.2.9: Audio-only (Live) 		Long text alternative with a text transcript of a prepared statement or script if the script is followed or use a real-time caption service
1.3 Adaptable
1.3.1 Info and Relationships A By using correct semantic HTML, information is conveyed to all users.
It helps: visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.3.1: Info and Relationships 		Semantic structure, color + text for form validation, ARIA
1.3.2 Meaningful Sequence A Strive to use the same DOM order as the visual order. A user with low vision who uses a screen magnifier in combination with a screen reader may be confused when the reading order appears to skip around on the screen. A keyboard user may have trouble predicting where focus will go next when the source order does not match the visual order.
It helps: visually impaired, motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.3.2: Meaningful Sequence 		DOM order == visual order, ordered lists vs unordered lists
1.3.3 Sensory Characteristics A The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that all users can access instructions for using the content, even when they cannot perceive shape or size or use information about spatial location or orientation
It helps: visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.3.3: Sensory Characteristics 		Avoid copy like "Read more in the right column", instead refer to a header. Avoid for example an arrow icon without complementary text.
1.3.4 Orientation AA Dont fix the design to one screen orientation, some users have their devices mounted in a fixed orientation (e.g. on the arm of a power wheelchair). An exception is if the content can only be understood or used in one screen orientation (for example a piano app).
It helps: motor impaired, visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.3.4: Orientation 		Allow landscape and portrait
1.3.5 Identify Input Purpose AA For some input fields, the type attribute already offers a way to broadly specify the intention of the input field, for example, input type="tel". The HTML autocomplete attribute takes this one step further, only accepting a certain number of specific well-defined fixed values. This allows a more fine-grained definition or identification of purpose than the type attribute.
It helps: cognitive impaired, motor impairments
WCAG: Understanding 1.3.5: Identify Input Purpose 		Use HTML 5 input types with autocomplete
1.3.6 Identify Purpose AAA Identifying regions of the page allows people to remove or highlight regions with their user agent.
It helps: cognitive impaired, visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.3.6: Identify Purpose 		Use aria landmarks.
Developers should always prefer using the correct semantic HTML element over using ARIA (for example the main element already has a role of main).
1.4 Distinguishable
1.4.1 Use of Color A Color is an important part of design, however, some users have difficulty perceiving color. The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that all users can access information that is conveyed by color differences.
It helps: visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.1: Use of Color 		Ensure that information conveyed by color is also available in text, for example form validation (dont just mark invalid fields with a red border) (remember programmatically set aria-invalid as well). Underline links or use a contrast ratio of 3:1.
1.4.2 Audio Control A Individuals who use screen reading software can find it hard to hear the speech output if there is other audio playing at the same time.
It helps: Visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.2: Audio Control 		Play sound for only 3 seconds. If longer, provide a way to stop it, or reduce the volyme to zero.
1.4.3 Contrast (Minimum) AA The intent of this Success Criterion is to provide enough contrast between text and its background so that it can be read by people with moderately low vision (who do not use contrast-enhancing assistive technology). The contrast requirement for larger text is therefore lower.
It helps: Visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.3: Contrast (Minimum) 		Text is less than 18 point if not bold and less than 14 point if bold = contrast ratio 4.5:1
Text is at least 18 point if not bold and at least 14 point if bold = contrast ratio 3:1
See tools.
Also remember including placeholder text. Text that is decorative and conveys no information is excluded, as are logos and disabled components.
1.4.4 Resize text AA The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that visually rendered text, including text-based controls, can be scaled successfully so that it can be read directly by people with mild visual disabilities
It helps: Visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.4: Resize text 		User should be able to zoom up to 200% without clipping, truncating or obscured content. Also use relative sizes like rem and percent, so user can change text size without zooming in images.
1.4.5 Images of Text AA If an author can use text to achieve the same visual effect, he or she should present the information as text rather than using an image. It helps people with low vision, so they can enlarge it or have it read by a screen reader. People with cognitive disabilities that affect reading are also helped.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.5: Images of Text 		Prefer text and CSS over images of text. Logos (and charts, screenshots) are an exception.
1.4.6 Contrast (Enhanced) AAA Text is less than 18 point if not bold and less than 14 point if bold = contrast ratio 7:1
Text is at least 18 point if not bold and at least 14 point if bold = contrast ratio 4.5:1
See tools
It helps: Visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.6: Contrast (Enhanced) 		Text that is decorative and conveys no information is excluded, as are logos and disabled components.
1.4.7 Low or No Background Audio AAA The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that any non-speech sounds are low enough that a user who is hard of hearing can separate the speech from background sounds.
It helps: Hearing impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.7: Low or No Background Audio 		Mixing audio files so that non-speech sounds are at least 20 decibels lower than the speech audio content
1.4.8 Visual Presentation AAA The intent of this AAA Success Criterion is to ensure that visually rendered text is presented in such a manner that it can be perceived without its layout interfering with its readability.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.8: Visual Presentation 		Foreground and background colors can be selected by the user. Text blocks width is no more than 80 characters. Text is not justified. Line height 1.5 and spacing between paragraphs is at least 1.5 larger than line height. Text can be resized without assistive technology up to 200 percent in a way that does not require the user to scroll horizontally to read a line of text.
1.4.9 Images of Text (No Exception) AAA Images of text are only used for pure decoration or where a particular presentation of text is essential to the information being conveyed.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.9: Images of Text (No Exception) 		Use text and CSS over images of text. Logos (and charts, screenshots) are an exception.
1.4.10 Reflow AA The intent of this Success Criterion is to support people with low vision who need to enlarge text and read it in a single column. Avoid scrolling in 2 dimensions:
Vertical scrolling content at a width equivalent to 320 CSS pixels (or browser window set to a width of 1280px and zoomed in to 400%),
Horizontal scrolling content at a height equivalent to 256 CSS pixels

It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.10: Reflow 		Make the site responsive. Data tables, some images and maps could be an exception.
1.4.11 Non-text Contrast AA The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that active user interface components (and state like selected and focus indicator) and meaningful graphics are distinguishable by people with moderately low vision. Active user interface component must have a minimum 3:1 contrast ratio with the adjacent colors.
It helps: Visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.11: Non-text Contrast 		See tools. Disabled components are an exception.
1.4.12 Text Spacing AA The developer should not hinder the end user changing the typography, and if user changes the typography to any of these bullet points the content should still be perceivable (no cut offs nor overlaps). Also the bullet points are a good baseline for developers to use themselves.
  • Line height to at least 1.5 times the font size
  • Spacing following paragraphs to at least 2 times the font size
  • Letter spacing (tracking) to at least 0.12 times the font size
  • Word spacing to at least 0.16 times the font size
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.12: Text Spacing 		See tools.
1.4.13 Content on Hover or Focus AA Content that apperars on hover and focus must be perceivable and dismissable.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired, motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.13: Content on Hover or Focus 		Close custom tooltips on ESC. Allow user to hover out on hover trigger without removing the triggered content. The triggered content should persist until user hovers out on the triggered content or closes it (status messages that changes are excempt, like a "busy" message thats no longer valid). HTML title are excempt. Content which can be triggered via pointer hover should also be able to be triggered by keyboard focus. Use role=tooltip.
2 Operable
2.1 Keyboard accessible
2.1.1 Keyboard A The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that, wherever possible, content can be operated through a keyboard.
It helps: Visually impaired, motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.1.1: Keyboard 		Ensure keyboard control for all functionality (there are exceptions like handdrawing). On mouseover event should also have an on focus event. Bind events to keyboard accessible controls like a, button, the user agent will take care of firing an onclick event for you if activated via the keyboard.
2.1.2 No keyboard trap A The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that content does not "trap" keyboard focus. If a user can move to a part of the page via the keyboard, the user must also be able to move away from it using only the keyboard. This is often an issue on pages that rely on plugins, such as Flash or Java applets, make sure the plugin can return keyboard focus.
It helps: Visually impaired, motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.1.2: No keyboard trap 		Ensuring that users are not trapped in content. The user should be able to tab away from a widget, or close it (like for example a modal) with ESC or a button.
2.1.3 Keyboard (no exception) AAA This is the same as Success Criterion 2.1.1, except that no exceptions are allowed. So, if an app prompts the user to do a hand drawing (path dependent), the app can not conform to this success criterion
It helps: Visually impaired, motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.1.3: Keyboard (no exception) 		See 2.1.1.
2.1.4 Character key shortcuts A The intent of this Success Crition is to reduce accidental activation of keyboard shortcuts that are made up of only character keys (no modifier). This can happen to keyboard users and users that use speech recognition.
It helps: Visually impaired, motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.1.4: Character key shortcuts 		If the site/app has single key shortcuts, let users turn them off or remap them. Note that this success criterion doesn't affect components such as listboxes and drop-down menus, that may be selected by one or more character keys, the shortcuts are only active when the components have focus. Accesskeys are also not affected because they include modifier keys.
2.2 Enough Time
2.2.1 Timing Adjustable A The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that users with disabilities are given adequate time to interact with Web content whenever possible. Any process that happens without user initiation after a set time or on a periodic basis is a time limit. This includes partial or full updates of content (for example, page refresh), changes to content, or the expiration of a window of opportunity for a user to react to a request for input. Also animated, moving or scrolling content introduces a time limit on a users ability to read content.
It helps: Visually impaired, motor impaired, hearing impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.2.1: Timing Adjustable 		User should be able to: turn off, adjust the length (10x default setting), request more time (20 seconds to do so, at least 10 times). Disabling time limits is better than customizing the length of time limits, which is better than requesting more time before a time limit occurs. There are exceptions, for example real time events like an auction. 20 hours+ time limit is also an exception.
2.2.2 Pause, Stop, Hide A The intent of this Success Criterion is to avoid distracting users during their interaction with a Web page. Partial content that moves, blinks, flashes, scrolls or auto updates, like animations, weather information or stock tickers can be distracting for people with certain disabilties.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.2.2: Pause, Stop, Hide 		Users should be able to pause, stop or hide partial content that is auto updating or moving, blinking or scrolling that lasts more than 5 seconds. An animation that occurs as part of a preload phase or similar situation can be considered an excempt if interaction cannot occur during that phase. Having an animation stop only so long as a user has focus on it (where it restarts as soon as the user moves the focus away) would not be considered a "mechanism for the user to pause" because it makes the page unusable in the process and would not meet this SC.
2.2.3 No Timing AAA The intent of this Success Criterion is to minimize the occurrence of content that requires timed interaction.
It helps: Visually impaired, motor impaired, hearing impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.2.3: No Timing 		Allow users to complete an activity without any time limit.
2.2.4 Interruptions AAA The intent of this Success Criterion is to allow users to turn off updates from the author/server except in emergencies (civil emergency alert messages or any other messages that warn of danger to health, safety, or property, including data loss, loss of connection, etcetera). This allows people with disabilities to focus on the content.
It helps: Cognitive impaired, visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.2.4: Interruptions 		Providing a mechanism to postpone any updating of content or providing a mechanism to request an update of the content instead of updating automatically. Or provide options for automatic notifications.
2.2.5 Re-authenticating AAA For security reasons, many sites implement an authentication time limit after a certain period of inactivity. These time limits may cause problems for persons with disabilities because it may take longer for them to complete the activity.
It helps: Cognitive impaired, motor impaired, visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.2.5: Re-authenticating 		Saving data so that it can be used after a user re-authenticates.
2.2.6 Timeouts AAA This Success Criterion works in tandem with Success Criterion 2.2.1 Timing Adjustable, but is specifically focused on notification of timeouts related to user inactivity. The use of timeouts can present barriers for users with disabilities, as these users may require more time to read content or to perform functions, such as completing an online form. This Success Criterion helps users by ensuring they are notified about timeouts related to inactivity.
It helps: Cognitive impaired, language related disabilities, motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.2.6: Timeouts 		Dont timeout the user for at least 20 hours, or store user data for more than 20 hours (is it really a timeout then?) or provide a warning of the timeout duration at the start of the process.
2.3 Seizures and Physical Reactions
2.3.1 Three Flashes or Below Threshold A Some users can experience seizures due to photosensitivity if content flashes at certain frequencies for more than a few flashes. People are even more sensitive to red flashing than to other colors. Blinking can be allowed for a short time as long as it stops (or can be stopped). Blinking usually does not occur at speeds of 3 per second or more, but it can. If blinking occurs faster than 3 per second, it would also be considered a flash.
It helps: Photosensitive epilepsy, photosensitive seizure disorders
WCAG: Understanding 2.3.1: Three Flashes or Below Threshold 		Ensure that no component of the content flashes more than 3 times in any 1-second period. If it does keep the area small enough and evaluate the pattern / red flash thresholds.
2.3.2 Three Flashes AAA The purpose of this Success Criterion is to further reduce the chance of seizures.
It helps: Photosensitive epilepsy, photosensitive seizure disorders
WCAG: Understanding 2.3.2: Three Flashes 		Whereas Success Criterion 2.3.1 allows flashing if it is dim enough or has a small enough area, Success Criterion 2.3.2 does not allow flashing greater than 3 per second, regardless of brightness or size.
2.3.3 Animation from Interactions AAA "Animation from interactions" applies when a users interaction initiates non-essential animation. In contrast, 2.2.2 Pause, Stop, Hide applies when the web page initiates animation. The intent of this Success Criterion is to allow users to prevent animation from interaction from being displayed on Web pages.
It helps: Vestibular (inner ear) disorder
WCAG: Understanding 2.3.3: Animation from Interactions 		Avoid using unnecessary animation. Provide a control for users to turn off non-essential animations from user interaction. Take advantage of the reduce motion feature in the user-agent or operating system, 'prefers-reduced-motion' CSS Media Query.
2.4 Navigable
2.4.1 Bypass blocks A The intent of this Success Criterion is to allow people who navigate sequentially through content more direct access to the primary content of the Web page, by skipping repeated blocks of content (like top navigation).
It helps: Visually impaired, motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.4.1: Bypass blocks 		Use proper semantics and landmarks. Provide skip links.
2.4.2 Page Titled A A descriptive title for each web page help users orient themselves on the web site. When titles appear in site maps or lists of search results, users can more quickly identify the content they need. User agents make the title of the page easily available to the user for identifying the page, by displaying it in the window or tab header.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired, motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.4.2: Page Titled 		Use descriptive titles that make sense when read out of context. Having link text and titles that agree, or be very similar, is good practice. Although not required for conformance, it could be a good idea to include the site name and sub sections in the title.
2.4.3 Focus Order A The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that when users navigate sequentially through content, they encounter information in an order that is consistent with the meaning of the content and can be operated from the keyboard.
It helps: Visually impaired, motor impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.4.3: Focus Order 		Use logical tab order (dont mess with tabindex 1,2,3). Develop accessible modals (see also 2.1.1). When the source order matches the visual order, everyone will read the content and interact with it in the same (correct) order. When using DOM scripting to insert content, insert the content immediately after the trigger control.
2.4.4 Link Purpose (In Context) A The intent of this Success Criterion is to help users understand the purpose of each link. The purpose of each link should be determined from the link text alone or from the link text together with its programmatically determined link context.
It helps: Visually impaired, motor impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.4.4: Link Purpose (In Context) 		The link text should describe the destination of the link. Make sure all links to the same destination use the same link text. Whenever possible, provide link text that identifies the purpose of the link without needing additional context. But in some situations the developer may want to provide part of the description of the link in logically related text that provides the context for the link, for example in a souranding p tag or td tag, aria-label or aria-labelledby can also be used (all of these are programmatically determined link context).
2.4.5 Multiple Ways AA Different users may find one technique of navigation more usable than others, therefor provide different ways of locating a web page.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.4.5: Multiple Ways 		Exception: where the Web Page is the result of, or a step in, a process. Consider: Providing a site map or table of contents. Consider providing search functionality. Consider providing links between pages as well as a main navigation.
2.4.6 Headings and Labels AA This Success Criterion does not require headings or labels. This Success Criterion requires that if headings or labels are provided, they be descriptive. Descriptive headings are especially helpful for users who have disabilities that make reading slow and for people with limited short-term memory. When heading and label have proper semantic, screen reader users can have them read out as a list and jump to them.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.4.6: Headings and Labels 		Headings should describe the content of their section. Labels should describe the purpose of their associated control.
2.4.7 Focus Visible AA The purpose of this success criterion is to help a person know which focusable element has the keyboard focus.
It helps: Visually impaired, motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.4.7: Focus Visible 		An exception to this is if there is only one focusable element on the page. Either let the browser default focus indicator do its thing (dont override it) or do override it with your own that match the design of the app/site (make it clear). A blinking cursor in an input field is a focus indicator on its own.
2.4.8 Location AAA The intent of this Success Criterion is to provide a way for the user to orient herself within a set of Web pages, a Web site, or a Web application and find related information.
It helps: Cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.4.8: Location 		Provide breadcrumbs, site map and for widgets, display whats active (for example an active tab in a set of tabs).
2.4.9 Link Purpose (Link Only) AAA A more strict version of 2.4.4. Link purpose should be clear when taken out of context (like a link list presented by a screen reader)
It helps: Visually impaired, motor impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.4.9: Link Purpose (Link Only) 		See 2.4.4, and also provide the link text that identifies the purpose of the link without needing additional context. aria-label can be sufficent as direct context ("in some assistive technologies the aria-label value will show in the list of links instead of the actual link text"), but it is better to write the text inside the a tag for meeting this success criteria.
2.4.10 Section Headings AAA The intent of this Success Criterion is to provide headings for sections of a Web page, when the page is organized into sections. Section tag can stand on its own but can also be part (like a chapter) of an Article element (see HTML sectioning flowchart under resources). When such sections exist, they need to have headings that introduce them. This provision is included at Level AAA because it cannot be applied to all types of content and it may not always be possible to insert headings, for example, when posting a pre-existing document to the Web.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.4.10: Section Headings 		Provide headings for each section. NOTE: "Sufficient Techniques" does not exemplify the section element, the author just uses h tags and p tags.
2.5 Input modalities
2.5.1 Pointer Gestures A Authors must ensure that their content can be operated without multipoint or path-based gestures such as swiping, dragging, pinching, or drawing. Multipoint or path-based gestures can be used so long as the functionality can also be operated by another method, such as a tap, click, double tap, double click, long press, or click and hold.
It helps: Motor impaired, cognitive impairded
WCAG: Understanding 2.5.1: Pointer Gestures 		Provide alternative ways of controlling an image gallery (swipe and buttons) or map (zoom with pinch and buttons) for example. If the gesture is considered essential (such as signing your name) its an exception.
2.5.2 Pointer Cancellation A The intent of this success criterion is to make it easier for users to prevent accidental or erroneous pointer input.
It helps: Motor impaired, cognitive impairded
WCAG: Understanding 2.5.2: Pointer Cancellation 		Trigger functionality on the mouse up event, so users can cancel the click/touch by mowing the pointer/finger way from the target before releasing.
2.5.3 Label in Name A For the purposes of this Success Criterion, "label" is simply referring to a text string in close visual proximity to a component. Avoid disconnect between the visible name (visible text in an element) and the programmatic name (like aria-label or alt text). For example, a text field has a [label] that says First name and an [aria-label] that says Your name, in this case, a screen reader would be presented with "Your Name" when they navigate into the field, and nothing will happen if a Dragon user says "Click First Name."
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired, motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.5.3: Label in Name 		If an element has both visual text and an accessible text, make sure that they communicate the same thing and the visual text is included in the accessible text. For example an image of an arrow with the word "Next" has an alt text with the word "Next" (and NOT for example continue).
2.5.4 Motion Actuation A When device motion or user motion (e.g., shaking, tilting, or gestures picked up by the devices camera) are used for interaction, an alternative input method should be provided to perform an equivalent action unless the motion is essential for the function and doing so would invalidate the activity.
It helps: Motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.5.4: Motion Actuation 		Provide more conventional controls as a complement to motion controls. The focus here is intentional gesturing by the user. It is not about for example position registered by geolocation sensors, nor does it cover motion associated with operating a keyboard, pointer, or assistive technology
2.5.5 Target Size AAA The intent of this success criteria is to ensure that target sizes are large enough for users to easily activate them, even if the user is accessing content on a small handheld touch screen device, has limited dexterity, or has trouble activating small targets for other reasons. For instance, mice and similar pointing devices can be hard to use for these users, and a larger target will help them activate the target.
It helps: Visually impaired, motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.5.5: Target Size 		Ensure that touch targets are at least 44 by 44 CSS pixels. A text link that is in a sentence or paragraph is excluded and does not need to meet the 44 by 44 CSS pixel requirement. Interfaces can still have controls smaller than 44 by 44. But, in that case, there must be another control to perform the same operation that is at least 44 by 44 pixels (or another way to perform the action like scrolling instead of using an anchor link).
2.5.6 Concurrent Input Mechanisms AAA The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that people can use and switch between different modes of input when interacting with web content.
It helps: Cognitive impaired, Visually impaired, motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.5.6: Concurrent Input Mechanisms 		Content must not limit the user's interaction to any particular input mechanism unless the restriction is essential, or is required to ensure the security of the content or to respect user settings. Dont focus solely on JavaScript event listeners when a touchscreen is detected
3 Understandable
3.1 Readable
3.1.1 Language of Page A Both assistive technologies and conventional user agents can render text more accurately when the language of the Web page is identified. Screen readers can load the correct pronunciation rules. Visual browsers can display characters and scripts (the writing system) correctly. Media players can show captions correctly. As a result, users with disabilities will be better able to understand the content.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 3.1.1: Language of Page 		Use the lang attribute for the HTML element. MDN: lang attributes format (parts)
3.1.2 Language of Parts AA The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that user agents can correctly present content written in multiple languages. This is especially important when switching between languages that read from left to right and languages that read from right to left, or when text is rendered in a language that uses a different alphabet. If there is doubt whether a change in language is intended, consider whether the word would be pronounced the same (except for accent or intonation) in the language of the immediately surrounding text.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 3.1.2: Language of Parts 		Use the lang attribute for the HTML element. And for tags where a switch of language is used.
3.1.3 Unusual Words AAA Certain disabilities make it difficult to understand some words or words used in an unusual way.
It helps: Cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 3.1.3: Unusual Words 		Link to a description list that explains words. MDN: The Description List element
3.1.4 Abbreviations AAA The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that users can access the expanded form of abbreviations.

3.1.5 Reading Level AAA Content should be written as clearly and simply as possible. Supplemental content is required when text demands reading ability more advanced than the lower secondary education level (more than nine years of school). It helps: Cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 3.1.5: Reading Level 		Simplify all text or provide a text summary (a h tag that says summary and p tags with the text (simpler language and avarage 16 words sentences)). Titles that refer to the name of documents, books, movies, etc are excempt.
3.1.6: Pronunciation AAA The intent of this Success Criterion is to help people who are blind, people who have low vision, and people with reading disabilities to understand content in cases where meaning depends on pronunciation. 3.2 Predictable
3.2.1: On Focus A Any component that is able to trigger an event when it receives focus must not change the context. For example: When a field receives focus, a help dialog window describing the field and providing options opens. As a keyboard user tabs through the Web page, the dialog opens, moving the keyboard focus away from the control every time the user attempts to tab past the field.
It helps: Visual impaired, cognitive impaired, motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 3.2.1: On Focus 		Change of content != change of context (changes of context, by their nature, interrupt the user by taking focus).
Opening a new window, moving focus to a different component, going to a new page (including anything that would look to a user as if they had moved to a new page) or significantly re-arranging the content of a page are examples of changes of context.
Tooltip that does not recieve focus is ok (but use a button with click event instead).
Dont remove focus programmatically.
3.2.2: On Input A The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that entering data or selecting a form control has predictable effects. Setting a form elements (as opposed to activating a link, or using a submit button to explicitly submit a form) shouldn't change the page context, unless the user is advised ahead of time.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 3.2.2: On Input 		Rather than changing the window.location or a language of a site when a select element is changed, add a submit button.
3.2.3: Consistent Navigation AA Use of consistent presentation and layout for users who interact with repeated content within a set of Web pages and need to locate specific information or functionality more than once. It is important to note that the use of the phrase "same order" in this section is not meant to imply that subnavigation menus cannot be used or that blocks of secondary navigation or page structure cannot be used.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 3.2.3: Consistent Navigation 		Place menus consistent in the same place. Place searchfields and skip to main content links in the same place.
3.2.4: Consistent Identification AA The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure consistent identification of functional components that appear repeatedly within a set of Web pages.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 3.2.4: Consistent Identification 		Keep labels, names, alt text consistent.
Make sure links to the same resource use the same link text.
Icons with similar functionality should follow a consistent labeling pattern. For example, "Print x" where x would be the thing to be printed.
Don't use a search submit button that says "Search" on some pages, and "Find" on others.
A check mark icon functions as "approved", on one page but as "included" on another. Since they serve different functions, they have different text alternatives.
Also prefer to group link and icon programmatically (and or aria-hidden on link)
3.2.5: Change on Request AAA The intent of this Success Criterion is to encourage design of Web content that gives users full control of changes of context. Content that initiates changes of context automatically only when user preferences allow can conform to this Success Criterion.
It helps: Visually impaired, motor impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 3.2.5: Change on Request 		Instead of automatically updating the content, the author provides an "Update now" button that requests a refresh of the content.
Let the user start a slide show by activating it
If a link opens in a new window (target blank), write it in the link text.
Be wary of posting data and render new data when user might not expect it, like when checking a checkbox.
3.3 Input Assistance
3.3.1 Error Identification A Users should be made aware that an error has occurred and can determine what is wrong. The error message should be as specific as possible. Per the definition in WCAG 2.0, an "input error" is information provided by the user that is not accepted: validation rules, required but omitted.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 3.3.1: Error Identification 		It is perfectly acceptable to indicate the error in other ways such as image, color etc, but errors must also be identified using text.
Identify input that has error programmatically with aria-invalid. aria-describedby can be used together with aria-invalid to programmatically connect error messages to the input that has the error.
ARIA21: Using Aria-Invalid to Indicate An Error Field (Example 2)
Can also place an element with role=alert before the form and put the validation messages there.
ARIA19: Using ARIA role=alert or Live Regions to Identify Errors

If using server side validation, it could be a good idea to return the page with the string "validation error" in the page title.
3.3.2 Labels or Instructions A The goal is to make certain that enough information is provided for the user to accomplish the task without undue confusion or navigation.
It helps: Cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 3.3.2: Labels or Instructions 		Use labels for inputs, fieldsets and legends, and connect them with for and id (see also 1.3.1: Info and Relationships).
Make sure form control labels are descriptive
Provide examples of the expected values and format (preferebly not in placeholder, since contrast issues (1.4.3 Contrast (Minimum)), put it in the label)


For data formats with common variations, such as dates and times, it may be useful to provide a preference option so users can use the format that is most comfortable to them.
G89: Providing expected data format and example
3.3.3 Error Suggestion AA The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that users receive appropriate suggestions for correction of an input error if it is possible. Some examples of information that is input error include information that is required but omitted by the user and information that is provided by the user but that falls outside the required data format or allowed values.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired, motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 3.3.3: Error Suggestion 		If inputs are required communicate it in text in the label, and use aria-required to reinforce it.
If a user tries to submit a form without populating the required inputs, see 3.3.1, and display a validation message, for example "Input data missing".
If a user tries to submit a form with format validation errors, see 3.3.1, and display a validation message, for example "Employee's Birth Date is after their hire date. Please verify the birth date and hire date."

Try to help the user with for example a type ahead for selecting a city.
See also 1.3.5: Identify Input Purpose (autocomplete).
3.3.4 Error Prevention (Legal, Financial, Data) AA The intent of this Success Criterion is to help users with disabilities avoid serious consequences as the result of a mistake when performing an action that cannot be reversed. For example, purchasing non-refundable airline tickets or submitting an order to purchase stock in a brokerage account are financial transactions with serious consequences. Also when deleting data about the user, error prevention should be used (it is not the intent to require a confirmation for each save command or the simple creation or editing of documents, records or other data).
It helps: Visually impaired, motor impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 3.3.4: Error Prevention (Legal, Financial, Data) 		Provide a statet time for when the action can be reversed.
provide the ability for the user to review and change the inputs.
Provide a checkbox for the user to confirm the input.
Ask for user confirmation before performing any irreversible actions.
3.3.5 Help AAA The intent of this Success Criterion is to help users avoid making mistakes. Some users with disabilities may be more likely to make mistakes than users without disabilities. Using context-sensitive help, users find out how to perform an operation without losing track of what they are doing.
It helps: Cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 3.3.5: Help 		Provide text instructions or help link. This could apply to both understanding content but also how to fill out forms (different input formats for example).
3.3.6 Error Prevention (All) AAA This criterion builds on Success Criterion 3.3.4 in that it applies to all forms that require users to submit information.
It helps: Visually impaired, motor impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 3.3.6: Error Prevention (All) 		Following the sufficient techniques for Success Criterion 3.3.4 for all forms that require the user to submit information.

4.1 Compatible
4.1.1 Parsing A Ensure that user agents, including assistive technologies, can accurately interpret and parse content. If the content cannot be parsed into a data structure, then different user agents may present it differently or be completely unable to parse it. Some user agents use "repair techniques" to render poorly coded content. Since repair techniques vary among user agents, authors cannot assume that content will be accurately parsed into a data structure.
It helps: Everyone
WCAG: Understanding 4.1.1: Parsing 		Use valid HTML. The W3C Markup Validation Service
4.1.2 Name, Role, Value A The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that Assistive Technologies (AT) can gather information about, activate(or set) and keep up to date on the status of user interface controls in the content. A particularly important state of a user interface control is whether or not it has focus. The focus state of a control can be programmatically determined, and notifications about change of focus are sent to user agents and assistive technology. Other examples of user interface control state are whether or not a checkbox or radio button has been selected, or whether or not a collapsible tree or list node is expanded or collapsed.
It helps: Visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 4.1.2: Name, Role, Value 		If standard controls are used in their default manner, this condition will be met. But custom widgets (of span/divs etc) should have name and role assigned (ARIA is used appropriately). Also use tabindex=0 to make it focusable.
If an image is changed via scripting, its alt text should be changed along with it.
4.1.3 Status Messages AA The intent of this Success Criterion is to make users aware of important changes in content that are not given focus, and to do so in a way that doesn't unnecessarily interrupt their work. The scope of this Success Criterion is specific to changes in content that involve status messages.

This Success Criterion specifically addresses scenarios where new content is added to the page without changing the user's context. Changes of context, by their nature, interrupt the user by taking focus. They are already surfaced by assistive technologies, and so have already met the goal to alert the user to new content.

Information can be added to pages which does not meet the definition of a status message. For example, the list of results obtained from a search are not considered a status update and thus are not covered by this Success Criterion. However, brief text messages displayed about the completion or status of the search, such as "Searching...", "18 results returned" or "No results returned" would be status updates if they do not take focus.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 4.1.3: Status Messages 		Make sure assistive technologies can recieve status messages that are not focusable (change of content).
Also communicate when status messages are hidden.
Use role=status.
Use aria-live=assertive or role=alert for error/warning messages.
role=alert is equivalent to using aria-live=assertive.
There is also aria-live=polite
MDN: ARIA live regions, string search for "Preferring specialized live region roles", also for "aria-atomic".

The container must be present in the DOM on page load for the message to be spoken by most screen readers. (<- string search)
