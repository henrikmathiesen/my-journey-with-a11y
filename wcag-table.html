My journey with a11y

Principle Guideline Success Criteria Conformance Understanding How to Meet
1 Perceivable
1.1 Text Alternatives
1.1.1 Non-text Content A Make information conveyed by non-text content accessible through the use of a text alternative. Text can be read visually, read out loud by assistive technology or be converted to braile.
It helps: Visually impaired, hearing impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.1.1: Non-text Content 		Short text alternative, Long text alternative, Alternatives for controls & inputs, Alternatives for time-based media, exams, or specific sensory experiences, CAPTCHA, Decorations or elements meant to be ignored by AT
1.2 Time-based Media
1.2.1 Audio-only and Video-only (Prerecorded) A Make information conveyed by non-text content accessible through the use of a text alternative. Text can be read visually, read out loud by assistive technology or be converted to braile. For prerecorded video content, authors have the option to provide an audio track.
It helps: Visually impaired, hearing impaired, cognitive impaired(?)
WCAG: Understanding 1.2.1: Audio-only and Video-only (Prerecorded) 		Long text alternative (text transcript), or provide a link to an audio version of the video-only presentation
1.2.2 Captions (Prerecorded) A The intent of this Success Criterion is to enable people who are deaf or hard of hearing to watch audio and video presentations. Captions are similar to dialogue-only subtitles except captions convey not only the content of spoken dialogue, but also equivalents for non-dialogue audio information needed to understand the program content, including sound effects, music, laughter, speaker identification and location.
It helps: hearing impaired, cognitive impaired(?)
WCAG: Understanding 1.2.2: Captions (Prerecorded) 		If you provide a video with sound, provide captions. The captions should include both speech and any other sounds that provide significant information.
1.2.3 Audio Description or Media Alternative (Prerecorded) A People who are blind or visually impaired should be able to access the visual information on audio and video presentation.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired(?)
WCAG: Understanding 1.2.3: Audio Description or Media Alternative (Prerecorded) 		Long text alternative (text transcript), or a supplemental audio track, with audible explanation of what's happening on screen, placed during existing pauses in dialog
1.2.4 Captions (Live) AA People who are deaf or hard of hearing are enabled to watch real-time presentations if captions provide the part of the content available via the audio track.
It helps: hearing impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.2.4: Captions (Live) 		Provide captioning for live media. Captions should include both dialogue and important sounds.
1.2.5 Audio Description (Prerecorded) AA The audio description augments the audio portion of the media presentation. People who are blind or have low vision as well as those with cognitive limitations who have difficulty interpreting visually what is happening will benefit.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.2.5: Audio Description (Prerecorded) 		Provide an additional audio track for prerecorded video content describing what is happening on-screen.
1.2.6 Sign Language (Prerecorded) AAA Written text, such as that found in captions, is often a second language to people who are deaf or hard of hearing. This AAA success criteria promotes the use of sign language translation of audio.
It helps: hearing impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.2.6: Sign Language (Prerecorded) 		Including a sign language interpreter in the video stream and make sure the interpreter is clearly visible.
1.2.7 Extended Audio Description (Prerecorded) AAA This AAA criteria takes into considaration that sometimes audio description is not enough, it needs to be extended.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.2.7: Extended Audio Description (Prerecorded) 		The media is paused between scenarios/problems and the extended audio description plays.
1.2.8 Media Alternative (Prerecorded) AAA This AAA critera focuses on those whose vision is too poor to reliably read captions and whose hearing is too poor to reliably hear dialogue and audio description. The alternative for time-based media reads something like a book.
It helps: Visually impaired, hearing impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.2.8: Media Alternative (Prerecorded) 		Long text alternative
1.2.9 Audio-only (Live) AAA A live text caption service will enable live audio to be accessible to people who are deaf or hard of hearing, or who cannot otherwise hear the audio. Such services use a trained human operator who listens in to what is being said and uses a special keyboard to enter the text with only a small delay.
It helps: hearing impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.2.9: Audio-only (Live) 		Long text alternative with a text transcript of a prepared statement or script if the script is followed or use a real-time caption service
1.3 Adaptable
1.3.1 Info and Relationships A By using correct semantic HTML, information is conveyed to all users.
It helps: visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.3.1: Info and Relationships 		Semantic structure, color + text for form validation, ARIA
1.3.2 Meaningful Sequence A Strive to use the same DOM order as the visual order. A user with low vision who uses a screen magnifier in combination with a screen reader may be confused when the reading order appears to skip around on the screen. A keyboard user may have trouble predicting where focus will go next when the source order does not match the visual order.
It helps: visually impaired, motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.3.2: Meaningful Sequence 		DOM order == visual order, ordered lists vs unordered lists
1.3.3 Sensory Characteristics A The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that all users can access instructions for using the content, even when they cannot perceive shape or size or use information about spatial location or orientation
It helps: visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.3.3: Sensory Characteristics 		Avoid copy like "Read more in the right column", instead refer to a header. Avoid for example an arrow icon without complementary text.
1.3.4 Orientation AA Dont fix the design to one screen orientation, some users have their devices mounted in a fixed orientation (e.g. on the arm of a power wheelchair). An exception is if the content can only be understood or used in one screen orientation (for example a piano app).
It helps: motor impaired, visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.3.4: Orientation 		Allow landscape and portrait
1.3.5 Identify Input Purpose AA For some input fields, the type attribute already offers a way to broadly specify the intention of the input field, for example, input type="tel". The HTML autocomplete attribute takes this one step further, only accepting a certain number of specific well-defined fixed values. This allows a more fine-grained definition or identification of purpose than the type attribute.
It helps: cognitive impaired, motor impairments
WCAG: Understanding 1.3.5: Identify Input Purpose 		Use HTML 5 input types with autocomplete
1.3.6 Identify Purpose AAA Identifying regions of the page allows people to remove or highlight regions with their user agent.
It helps: cognitive impaired, visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.3.6: Identify Purpose 		Use aria landmarks.
Developers should always prefer using the correct semantic HTML element over using ARIA (for example the main element already has a role of main).
1.4 Distinguishable
1.4.1 Use of Color A Color is an important part of design, however, some users have difficulty perceiving color. The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that all users can access information that is conveyed by color differences.
It helps: visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.1: Use of Color 		Ensure that information conveyed by color is also available in text, for example form validation (dont just mark invalid fields with a red border) (remember programmatically set aria-invalid as well). Underline links or use a contrast ratio of 3:1.
1.4.2 Audio Control A Individuals who use screen reading software can find it hard to hear the speech output if there is other audio playing at the same time.
It helps: Visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.2: Audio Control 		Play sound for only 3 seconds. If longer, provide a way to stop it, or reduce the volyme to zero.
1.4.3 Contrast (Minimum) AA The intent of this Success Criterion is to provide enough contrast between text and its background so that it can be read by people with moderately low vision (who do not use contrast-enhancing assistive technology). The contrast requirement for larger text is therefore lower.
It helps: Visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.3: Contrast (Minimum) 		Text is less than 18 point if not bold and less than 14 point if bold = contrast ratio 4.5:1
Text is at least 18 point if not bold and at least 14 point if bold = contrast ratio 3:1
See tools.
Also remember including placeholder text. Text that is decorative and conveys no information is excluded, as are logos and disabled components.
1.4.4 Resize text AA The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that visually rendered text, including text-based controls, can be scaled successfully so that it can be read directly by people with mild visual disabilities
It helps: Visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.4: Resize text 		User should be able to zoom up to 200% without clipping, truncating or obscured content. Also use relative sizes like rem and percent, so user can change text size without zooming in images.
1.4.5 Images of Text AA If an author can use text to achieve the same visual effect, he or she should present the information as text rather than using an image. It helps people with low vision, so they can enlarge it or have it read by a screen reader. People with cognitive disabilities that affect reading are also helped.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.5: Images of Text 		Prefer text and CSS over images of text. Logos (and charts, screenshots) are an exception.
1.4.6 Contrast (Enhanced) AAA Text is less than 18 point if not bold and less than 14 point if bold = contrast ratio 7:1
Text is at least 18 point if not bold and at least 14 point if bold = contrast ratio 4.5:1
See tools
It helps: Visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.6: Contrast (Enhanced) 		Text that is decorative and conveys no information is excluded, as are logos and disabled components.
1.4.7 Low or No Background Audio AAA The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that any non-speech sounds are low enough that a user who is hard of hearing can separate the speech from background sounds.
It helps: Hearing impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.7: Low or No Background Audio 		Mixing audio files so that non-speech sounds are at least 20 decibels lower than the speech audio content
1.4.8 Visual Presentation AAA The intent of this AAA Success Criterion is to ensure that visually rendered text is presented in such a manner that it can be perceived without its layout interfering with its readability.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.8: Visual Presentation 		Foreground and background colors can be selected by the user. Text blocks width is no more than 80 characters. Text is not justified. Line height 1.5 and spacing between paragraphs is at least 1.5 larger than line height. Text can be resized without assistive technology up to 200 percent in a way that does not require the user to scroll horizontally to read a line of text.
1.4.9 Images of Text (No Exception) AAA Images of text are only used for pure decoration or where a particular presentation of text is essential to the information being conveyed.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.9: Images of Text (No Exception) 		Use text and CSS over images of text. Logos (and charts, screenshots) are an exception.
1.4.10 Reflow AA The intent of this Success Criterion is to support people with low vision who need to enlarge text and read it in a single column. Avoid scrolling in 2 dimensions:
Vertical scrolling content at a width equivalent to 320 CSS pixels (or browser window set to a width of 1280px and zoomed in to 400%),
Horizontal scrolling content at a height equivalent to 256 CSS pixels

It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.10: Reflow 		Make the site responsive. Data tables, some images and maps could be an exception.
1.4.11 Non-text Contrast AA The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that active user interface components (and state like selected and focus indicator) and meaningful graphics are distinguishable by people with moderately low vision. Active user interface component must have a minimum 3:1 contrast ratio with the adjacent colors.
It helps: Visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.11: Non-text Contrast 		See tools. Disabled components are an exception.
1.4.12 Text Spacing AA The developer should not hinder the end user changing the typography, and if user changes the typography to any of these bullet points the content should still be perceivable (no cut offs nor overlaps). Also the bullet points are a good baseline for developers to use themselves.
  • Line height to at least 1.5 times the font size
  • Spacing following paragraphs to at least 2 times the font size
  • Letter spacing (tracking) to at least 0.12 times the font size
  • Word spacing to at least 0.16 times the font size
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.12: Text Spacing 		See tools.
1.4.13 Content on Hover or Focus AA Content that apperars on hover and focus must be perceivable and dismissable.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired, motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 1.4.13: Content on Hover or Focus 		Close custom tooltips on ESC. Allow user to hover out on hover trigger without removing the triggered content. The triggered content should persist until user hovers out on the triggered content or closes it (status messages that changes are excempt, like a "busy" message thats no longer valid). HTML title are excempt. Content which can be triggered via pointer hover should also be able to be triggered by keyboard focus. Use role=tooltip.
2 Operable
2.1 Keyboard accessible
2.1.1 Keyboard A The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that, wherever possible, content can be operated through a keyboard.
It helps: Visually impaired, motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.1.1: Keyboard 		Ensure keyboard control for all functionality (there are exceptions like handdrawing). On mouseover event should also have an on focus event. Bind events to keyboard accessible controls like a, button, the user agent will take care of firing an onclick event for you if activated via the keyboard.
2.1.2 No keyboard trap A The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that content does not "trap" keyboard focus. If a user can move to a part of the page via the keyboard, the user must also be able to move away from it using only the keyboard. This is often an issue on pages that rely on plugins, such as Flash or Java applets, make sure the plugin can return keyboard focus.
It helps: Visually impaired, motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.1.2: No keyboard trap 		Ensuring that users are not trapped in content. The user should be able to tab away from a widget, or close it (like for example a modal) with ESC or a button.
2.1.3 Keyboard (no exception) AAA This is the same as Success Criterion 2.1.1, except that no exceptions are allowed. So, if an app prompts the user to do a hand drawing (path dependent), the app can not conform to this success criterion
It helps: Visually impaired, motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.1.3: Keyboard (no exception) 		See 2.1.1.
2.1.4 Character key shortcuts A The intent of this Success Crition is to reduce accidental activation of keyboard shortcuts that are made up of only character keys (no modifier). This can happen to keyboard users and users that use speech recognition.
It helps: Visually impaired, motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.1.4 Character key shortcuts 		If the site/app has single key shortcuts, let users turn them off or remap them. Note that this success criterion doesn't affect components such as listboxes and drop-down menus, that may be selected by one or more character keys, the shortcuts are only active when the components have focus. Accesskeys are also not affected because they include modifier keys.
2.2 Enough Time
2.2.1 Timing Adjustable A The intent of this Success Criterion is to ensure that users with disabilities are given adequate time to interact with Web content whenever possible. Any process that happens without user initiation after a set time or on a periodic basis is a time limit. This includes partial or full updates of content (for example, page refresh), changes to content, or the expiration of a window of opportunity for a user to react to a request for input. Also animated, moving or scrolling content introduces a time limit on a users ability to read content.
It helps: Visually impaired, motor impaired, hearing impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.2.1: Timing Adjustable 		User should be able to: turn off, adjust the length (10x default setting), request more time (20 seconds to do so, at least 10 times). Disabling time limits is better than customizing the length of time limits, which is better than requesting more time before a time limit occurs. There are exceptions, for example real time events like an auction. 20 hours+ time limit is also an exception.
2.2.2 Pause, Stop, Hide A The intent of this Success Criterion is to avoid distracting users during their interaction with a Web page. Partial content that moves, blinks, flashes, scrolls or auto updates, like animations, weather information or stock tickers can be distracting for people with certain disabilties.
It helps: Visually impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.2.2: Pause, Stop, Hide 		Users should be able to pause, stop or hide partial content that is auto updating or moving, blinking or scrolling that lasts more than 5 seconds. An animation that occurs as part of a preload phase or similar situation can be considered an excempt if interaction cannot occur during that phase. Having an animation stop only so long as a user has focus on it (where it restarts as soon as the user moves the focus away) would not be considered a "mechanism for the user to pause" because it makes the page unusable in the process and would not meet this SC.
2.2.3 No Timing AAA The intent of this Success Criterion is to minimize the occurrence of content that requires timed interaction.
It helps: Visually impaired, motor impaired, hearing impaired, cognitive impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.2.3: No Timing 		Allow users to complete an activity without any time limit.
2.2.4 Interruptions AAA The intent of this Success Criterion is to allow users to turn off updates from the author/server except in emergencies (civil emergency alert messages or any other messages that warn of danger to health, safety, or property, including data loss, loss of connection, etcetera). This allows people with disabilities to focus on the content.
It helps: Cognitive impaired, visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.2.4: Interruptions 		Providing a mechanism to postpone any updating of content or providing a mechanism to request an update of the content instead of updating automatically. Or provide options for automatic notifications.
2.2.5 Re-authenticating AAA For security reasons, many sites implement an authentication time limit after a certain period of inactivity. These time limits may cause problems for persons with disabilities because it may take longer for them to complete the activity.
It helps: Cognitive impaired, motor impaired, visually impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.2.5: Re-authenticating 		Saving data so that it can be used after a user re-authenticates.
2.2.6 Timeouts AAA This Success Criterion works in tandem with Success Criterion 2.2.1 Timing Adjustable, but is specifically focused on notification of timeouts related to user inactivity. The use of timeouts can present barriers for users with disabilities, as these users may require more time to read content or to perform functions, such as completing an online form. This Success Criterion helps users by ensuring they are notified about timeouts related to inactivity.
It helps: Cognitive impaired, language related disabilities, motor impaired
WCAG: Understanding 2.2.6: Timeouts 		Dont timeout the user for at least 20 hours, or store user data for more than 20 hours (is it really a timeout then?) or provide a warning of the timeout duration at the start of the process.
